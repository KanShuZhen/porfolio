3cat.my

3cat.my is a Malaysia-based e-commerce platform for secondhand Apple devices. Backed by 21 physical stores nationwide, it blends online convenience with offline trust. Customers can browse, purchase, and get support through chat, while enjoying benefits like installment plans, warranties, and verified sellers—ensuring an affordable and reliable way to own quality Apple products.

Context

We began noticing a growing trend where users frequently requested a feature that would allow multiple payment methods in one order. For example, paying part of the amount via ShopeePay and the remaining via online banking. While we initially saw this as a “nice-to-have,” it soon became clear that the manual handling of these split transactions overwhelmed our operations team, causing order delays and more support tickets. This raised a key question that was it just a feature users wanted, or a need they couldn’t ignore?

Role

Product designer and user researcher

Discover, Design and Deliver

User Interview

To further understand the real needs behind split payments, I followed up with 5 recent buyers who had recently shopped on 3cat.my and agreed to share their experiences. My interviewees included both male and female customers, ranging from university students to working professionals, with varying levels of familiarity with e-wallets and online banking.

Key Insights

  • Cash flow management was the real driver as users didn’t necessarily want to split between ShopeePay and online banking, they simply didn’t have enough balance in one of the method.
  • User want to maximize cashback rewards e.g.credit monthly cashback reward or ShopeePay.
  • The manual coordination required to process these orders felt complicated, especially for first-time users who were already nervous about buying secondhand devices online.
  • Some users feared losing their order or payment if things didn’t go through properly.

“It’s not that I want to split payment. I just need options when my bank balance is tight.”

“I’ll use my credit card for part of it so I can hit the monthly minimum spend for cashback, and then pay the rest with online banking.”

“When the system doesn’t allow split payments, I have to contact support and wait. I just want a smoother way to do it myself.”

Define

Problem Statement

How might we design a checkout experience that enables flexible split payments while reducing operational friction?

Goals

User Goals

  • Pay flexibly using two methods without confusion.
  • Avoid order delays caused by manual processing.
  • Feel confident their transaction is secure and confirmed instantly.

Business Goals

  • Reduce manual intervention for split transactions.
  • Lower the volume of support tickets related to payment issues.
  • Increase checkout completion rates and customer satisfaction.

Design

Ideation

We held a collaborative brainstorming session using “How Might We” prompts and affinity mapping. This helped us reframe user pain points into opportunities.

From the discussion, several key ideas emerged:

  • Allow a native split payment option directly within the checkout flow.
  • Providing real-time feedback on available balances.
  • Simplified split-payment reconciliation. When users split payments (e.g. part credit card and part e-wallet), the backend can become complex, leading to issues like double counting, delayed settlements, or confusing statuses. To avoid this, we designed a clearer system by showing a transparent breakdown of payment methods, syncing processors in real-time, and generating one unified confirmation ID so users and merchants see it as a single order. This simplification ensured smoother operations for merchants and a single, easy-to-understand record for users.

Wireframe

Usability Testing

To validate our split-payment flow early without heavy resource investment, I conducted guerrilla usability testing with few participants. This lean approach allowed us to gather immediate feedback on whether users could intuitively complete a split transaction. Observing their interactions surfaced critical friction points such as unclear balance indicators and confusing button hierarchy which we refined to ensure the checkout flow felt simpler and more trustworthy.

Deliver

I delivered the design specifications for the initial product version. Usability tests are now underway and I’ll refine the design in the next iteration based on user feedback.

Impact

  • Reduced manual payment handling workload by 60%, validated through support ticket reviews and backend reconciliation, allowing the team to focus on higher-value tasks.
  • Within the first month post-launch, checkout completion rates improved by 12% among users who had previously abandoned due to payment concerns.

Reflections

This project established a foundation for future multi-wallet integrations and showed me how a single feature request can reveal deeper financial behavior patterns. I learned that user wants often point to more fundamental needs, such as flexibility and financial control. At the same time, I saw how small operational pain points can quickly scale into larger inefficiencies if left unaddressed. By balancing user flexibility with operational efficiency, we were able to create a scalable solution that benefits both customers and the business. I also realized how essential clear guidance and feedback are when designing multi-step financial flows, as they help users stay confident and informed throughout the process.
